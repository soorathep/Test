Karl Popper transformed the philosophy of science with a remarkably simple idea.

Science advances not by proving ideas right, but by discovering where they fail.

At first, this sounds counterintuitive.

Researchers spend years developing hypotheses, designing experiments, collecting data, and writing papers. Naturally, we hope our ideas will be supported.

Yet Popper would probably ask a different question.

> How could you find out that your hypothesis is wrong?

That question changes everything.

Many of us begin a project hoping to confirm what we already believe.

> We search for evidence that supports our hypothesis.  
> We feel encouraged when the data align with our expectations.  
> We feel disappointed when they do not.

But science asks something more demanding.

It asks us to challenge our own ideas with the same honesty that we would challenge someone else's.

A hypothesis is valuable not because it explains existing observations.

A hypothesis becomes scientific because it can be tested—and because it can, in principle, be shown to be wrong.

This is what makes research different from advocacy.

Our responsibility is not to defend an idea.

Our responsibility is to discover whether the idea survives careful examination.

That is not always easy.

> When experiments fail, we often feel that we have failed.  
> When reviewers criticize our interpretation, we instinctively prepare a defense.  
> When results contradict years of work, the temptation is to search for reasons why the data must be mistaken.

But perhaps these moments deserve a different interpretation.

> A failed experiment is not always a failed study.  
> A critical reviewer is not always an obstacle.  
> Unexpected data are not always bad news.

Sometimes they are the first signs that reality is teaching us something our hypothesis could not.

The history of science is filled with ideas that endured for years before better evidence replaced them.

Progress did not occur because scientists protected those ideas.

Progress occurred because someone was willing to ask a difficult question, perform a decisive experiment, and accept the answer.

Perhaps this is the quiet courage that Popper asks of every researcher.

Not the courage to defend our theories.

But the courage to test them where they are most likely to fail.

This does not diminish the importance of creativity.

Every important discovery begins with imagination.

But imagination alone is not enough.

Our best ideas earn their place only after they have survived our most rigorous attempts to disprove them.

Perhaps the greatest mistake in research is not being wrong.

It is holding an idea in a way that no evidence could ever challenge.

Every morning, a researcher should ask one simple question:

> What evidence would convince me that I am wrong?

Science moves forward not because we avoid being wrong.

It moves forward because we are willing to discover when we are.
